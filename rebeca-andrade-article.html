Brazil's Rebeca Andrade: the gymnast who refused to give up

Based on verified sources · July 2026

Most people don't know where Rebeca Andrade grew up. The answer might surprise them. She was born in Guarulhos, a city just outside São Paulo, in a favela where eight children shared a home with a single mother who worked as a maid. There were no coaches, no facilities, no sponsorships. There was a bunk bed.

"I would go up, go down, and hang," Andrade once recalled in an interview. "I think I was already training and I didn't even know."

She was four years old when her older brother Emerson started taking her to a local gymnastics gym — on his bicycle, because the family couldn't afford public transport. Their mother, Rosa, walked to work every morning so that Rebeca could use her bus pass to get to practice.

"My mother walked to work so that I could use her bus pass to go to the gym with my brother. I am very grateful to her, and to my siblings, who made my dream possible."

— Rebeca Andrade

A Road Full of Obstacles

But the road to the Olympics was anything but smooth.

Do you have any idea how many times she tore her ACL (anterior cruciate ligament — one of the main ligaments in the knee)? Three. In 2015, 2017, and 2019 — three separate knee injuries that would have ended most athletes' careers before they truly began. Each time, the questions came: Could you tell me if she'll ever compete again? Do you know when — or whether — she'll come back?

Each time, she did.

Tokyo 2021: History Is Made

At the Tokyo Olympics in 2021, Andrade made history. She became the first Brazilian woman to win a gymnastics medal at the Olympic Games — and she didn't just win one. She took gold on vault and silver in the all-around. An entire country celebrated. Don't they say that resilience is the most important quality in a champion? Andrade proved it.

Paris 2024: Four Medals and a Flag

But 2024 was something else entirely.

At the Paris Olympics, Andrade won four medals: a team bronze, two silvers, and — in front of a roaring crowd — a gold medal on the floor exercise. She performed her routine to a Brazilian funk song, and the audience could not stay still. When it was over, she was chosen to carry the Brazilian flag at the closing ceremony, a moment that reduced her family, her coaches, and millions of fans back home to tears.

She is now one of only three gymnasts in the 21st century to have won medals on every single apparatus at the World Championships. The other two? Simone Biles and Aliya Mustafina. In April 2025, she won the Laureus World Sports Award for Comeback of the Year.

"Would you mind telling me what drives someone through three ACL surgeries, a childhood without resources, and the pressure of representing an entire nation?"

— A journalist at a Paris 2024 press conference

Andrade's answer was simple. She still thinks about the bunk bed.

📖 Word Bank

favela
An informal neighborhood, usually on the outskirts of a Brazilian city, where people with lower incomes live.
ACL (anterior cruciate ligament)
One of the main ligaments in the knee. A tear is a serious sports injury that requires surgery and months of recovery.
resilience
The ability to recover quickly from difficulties; the strength to keep going after failure or setbacks.
all-around
A gymnastics competition where athletes compete on all events — vault, bars, beam, and floor.
apparatus
The equipment used in gymnastics, such as the vault, the uneven bars, the balance beam, and the floor mat.
obstacle
Something that makes it hard to move forward; a problem or challenge that blocks your path.
sponsorship
Financial support given to an athlete by a company, usually in exchange for advertising or publicity.
Laureus Award
One of the most prestigious awards in world sport, given each year to recognize outstanding athletic achievements.
